Overview

Given the rapid pace in technology advancements, combined with the goal of digitizing the citizen experience, one of the biggest hurdles to success is to break away from the "business as usual" headspace within the organization itself.

Within government, authors are, or work with, the subject matters experts (SMEs). Management is the authority. Historically, those groups have had the ability to create and host content within the structure and labelling model they preferred. This resulted in content being organized by the section, branch or department that is within their span of control.

While this made content creation and maintenance easier, it wasn't supporting a topic and task-based IA model inline with users’ needs.

Areas that covered the same topic or task were creating competing pages. This meant the users would need to know about:

  • which area was responsible for specific information in the first place
  • all the pages created across different areas of organization or different organizations, review them, and piece together the full story

While Treasury Board has introduced a single IA for public facing content on Canada.ca, most government intranets still organize content by owner and not by topic. This is a problem, since to improve findability and discoverability of information, it should be organized by task rather than program area.

It's also important to remember that all internal resources provided to employees impact and are part of user experience on the other end. Just because a resource is internal and not public-facing, it doesn't mean that it has a lesser impact on our citizens.

Changes needed

In order to create effective, meaningful, flexible and lasting information architecture that supports users and helps government deliver its objectives, a number of things need to change:

  • How we think about information
  • How we work with information
  • How we govern information
